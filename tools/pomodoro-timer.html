ü§î‚ùìWhat is Pomodoro Timer?

A Pomodoro Timer is a productivity tool based on the Pomodoro Technique, a time management method developed by Francesco Cirillo in the late 1980s. It is designed to help you maintain laser-like focus by breaking your workday into manageable chunks, preventing the "marathon" burnout that often happens when you try to work for hours on end.


ü•´Why is it called "Pomodoro"?

The name comes from the Italian word for tomato. As a university student, Cirillo used a tomato-shaped kitchen timer to track his work sessions. He found that committing to just 10 minutes of "undistracted" time helped him overcome procrastination. Eventually, he settled on the 25-minute rhythm used today.

The standard "Pomodoro" cycle consists of four main phases:

  • The Pomodoro (25 min): Work on a single task with zero distractions. No emails, no phone, no "quick" checks of social media.
  • Short Break (5 min): When the timer rings, you must stop. Step away from your desk, stretch, or grab water.
  • The Repeat: After your break, start another 25-minute Pomodoro.
  • The Long Break (15‚Äì30 min): After completing four Pomodoros, take a much longer break to fully recharge your brain.

‚ùìFrequently Asked Questions


Question Answer
Why 25 minutes? It's long enough to get meaningful work done but short enough to maintain intense focus without burnout.
What do I do during the breaks? Step away from your screen! Stretch, grab water, or meditate. The goal is to give your brain a total rest.
Can I change the timer lengths? Absolutely. While 25/5 is the classic rhythm, many people prefer "Flowtime" (50/10) if they are doing deep creative work.
What if I get interrupted? If a distraction is unavoidable, end the Pomodoro there and start over later. The goal is "unbroken" focus.
